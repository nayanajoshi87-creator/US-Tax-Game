PART 2 — US TAX CONCEPT DEEP DIVES

FORM 1040 — U.S. Individual Income Tax Return
Definition: Form 1040 is the standard federal income tax return filed by US individual taxpayers. Every other schedule and form ultimately feeds into Form 1040.
Purpose: To calculate the taxpayer's total income, deductions, credits, tax liability, and determine the refund or amount owed.
The Tax Formula — Memorize This
FORM 1040 FLOW (Top to Bottom)
• GROSS INCOME: All income from all sources (wages, interest, dividends, business, rental, etc.)
• MINUS: Above-the-line adjustments (Schedule 1, Part II): student loan interest, HSA, IRA, alimony (pre-2019), etc.
• = ADJUSTED GROSS INCOME (AGI): The single most important number on the return
• MINUS: Standard Deduction OR Itemized Deductions (Schedule A) — whichever is GREATER
• MINUS: Qualified Business Income Deduction (Section 199A) if applicable
• = TAXABLE INCOME
• APPLY: Tax rates (2024 brackets) to taxable income = Gross Tax Liability
• MINUS: Non-refundable credits (CTC, child care, education credits, foreign tax credit)
• MINUS: Refundable credits (EIC, Additional CTC, Premium Tax Credit)
• PLUS: Other taxes (SE tax, NIIT, IRMAA, AMT, early withdrawal penalty)
• MINUS: Payments (withholding, estimated tax payments)
• = REFUND or AMOUNT DUE
Key Lines Every Preparer Must Know
•	Line 1a: W-2 wages (from Box 1 of W-2)
•	Line 2b: Taxable interest income
•	Line 3b: Ordinary dividends
•	Line 4b: IRA distributions (taxable portion)
•	Line 5b: Pension and annuity distributions (taxable portion)
•	Line 6b: Social Security benefits (taxable portion — 0%, 50%, or 85%)
•	Line 7: Capital gain or loss (from Schedule D)
•	Line 8: Additional income (from Schedule 1, Part I)
•	Line 11: AGI — sum of all income minus Schedule 1 adjustments
•	Line 12: Standard or itemized deduction
•	Line 15: Taxable income
•	Line 16: Tax from tax tables or Schedule D Tax Worksheet
•	Line 17: Alternative Minimum Tax (Form 6251)
•	Line 19: Child Tax Credit / Credit for Other Dependents
•	Line 27: Earned Income Credit
•	Line 33: Total payments (withholding + estimated + refundable credits)
•	Line 35a: Refund amount
•	Line 37: Amount owed

SCHEDULE 1 — Additional Income and Adjustments
Definition: Schedule 1 is attached to Form 1040 to report income and deductions not listed directly on the main form.
Part I: Additional Income (flows to Form 1040 Line 8)
•	Line 1: Taxable refunds of state/local taxes (only if taxpayer itemized in prior year)
•	Line 2a/2b: Alimony received (only divorce agreements before 2019)
•	Line 3: Business income/loss (from Schedule C)
•	Line 4: Other gains/losses (from Form 4797)
•	Line 5: Rental real estate, royalties, partnerships (from Schedule E)
•	Line 6: Farm income/loss (Schedule F)
•	Line 8: Other additional income (gambling winnings, jury duty pay, hobby income)
Part II: Above-the-Line Adjustments (flows to AGI)
ABOVE-THE-LINE DEDUCTIONS — CRITICAL LIST
• Line 11: Educator expenses — up to $300 ($600 MFJ if both teachers) — does NOT require itemizing
• Line 12: Business expenses (military reservists, performing artists, fee-based government officials)
• Line 13: Health Savings Account (HSA) deduction — Form 8889, Part I
• Line 17: Self-employed health insurance deduction
• Line 15: Deductible part of self-employment tax (Schedule SE, Line 13) — exactly 50% of SE tax
• Line 16: Self-employed SEP, SIMPLE, and qualified plan contributions
• Line 19: Alimony paid (pre-2019 divorce only; NOT for post-2018 divorces)
• Line 20: IRA deduction (if eligible — depends on AGI and workplace plan participation)
• Line 21: Student loan interest deduction — phases out at higher AGI
• Line 24: Other adjustments (jury duty pay given to employer, etc.)

SCHEDULE A — Itemized Deductions
Definition: Schedule A allows taxpayers to deduct specific expenses instead of the standard deduction. Only beneficial when total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction.
2024 Standard Deduction Amounts — Must Memorize
•	Single / MFS: $14,600
•	Married Filing Jointly (MFJ): $29,200
•	Head of Household: $21,900
•	Additional for age 65+ or blind: $1,550 (MFJ) or $1,950 (single/HoH)
Key Categories on Schedule A
ITEMIZED DEDUCTIONS — LINE BY LINE
• Medical & Dental (Lines 1-4): Only expenses EXCEEDING 7.5% of AGI are deductible
• State & Local Taxes — SALT (Lines 5-7): LIMITED to $10,000 total ($5,000 MFS) — includes state income OR sales tax + property taxes
• Home Mortgage Interest (Lines 8-8b): Interest on acquisition debt up to $750,000 ($375,000 MFS) for loans after 12/15/2017
• Investment Interest (Line 9): Interest on funds borrowed to buy investments — limited to net investment income
• Charitable Contributions (Lines 11-14): Cash gifts require receipt for $250+; non-cash over $500 needs Form 8283
• Casualty & Theft Losses (Line 15): ONLY for federally declared disasters after TCJA 2017
• Other Itemized Deductions (Lines 16): Gambling losses (only to extent of winnings), impairment-related work expenses
INTERVIEW TRAP	Interviewers often ask: 'When would you recommend itemizing over the standard deduction?' Answer: When Schedule A total > standard deduction. Common triggers — high mortgage interest, large charitable gifts, high state/local taxes (but capped at $10K), significant medical expenses above 7.5% floor.

SCHEDULE C — Profit or Loss from Business (Sole Proprietorship)
Definition: Schedule C is used by self-employed individuals and sole proprietors to report income and deductions from a business they own and operate.
Who Files Schedule C
•	Sole proprietors (no LLC or partnership)
•	Single-member LLCs (unless elected S-Corp or C-Corp treatment)
•	Independent contractors, freelancers, gig workers (Uber, DoorDash, Upwork)
Key Lines
•	Line 1: Gross receipts or sales
•	Line 2: Returns and allowances
•	Line 4: Cost of goods sold
•	Line 7: Gross profit
•	Line 28: Total expenses
•	Line 31: Net profit or (loss) — flows to Schedule 1, Line 3
Common Deductible Expenses on Schedule C
SCHEDULE C EXPENSE CATEGORIES
• Advertising: Fully deductible
• Car and truck expenses: Either actual expenses or standard mileage rate (2024: 67 cents/mile)
• Commissions and fees: Fully deductible
• Depreciation (Form 4562): Section 179 expensing and bonus depreciation
• Home office deduction: Regular and exclusive use — simplified method ($5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500) or actual expense method
• Legal and professional services: Fully deductible
• Office expenses: Supplies, postage
• Wages paid to employees: Deductible (but not owner's own salary)
• Meals: 50% deductible (2024 — business meals with clients)
• Travel: Fully deductible for business travel away from home
Self-Employment Tax — Critical Calculation
Net profit from Schedule C triggers Self-Employment Tax (SE Tax). This compensates for the fact that self-employed individuals pay BOTH the employee and employer portions of FICA.
•	SE Tax rate: 15.3% on first $168,600 of net SE income (2024), then 2.9% above that
•	Calculation: Net profit × 92.35% (to account for employer half deduction) × 15.3%
•	Deduction: 50% of SE tax is deducted on Schedule 1, Line 15 (above-the-line)
•	Reported on: Schedule SE, then flows to Form 1040

SCHEDULE D — Capital Gains and Losses
Definition: Schedule D is used to report sales and exchanges of capital assets — primarily stocks, bonds, real estate, and other investments.
Short-Term vs Long-Term — The Fundamental Distinction
CAPITAL GAINS HOLDING PERIOD RULES
• SHORT-TERM: Asset held 1 year or LESS — taxed at ORDINARY income tax rates (up to 37% in 2024)
• LONG-TERM: Asset held MORE than 1 year — taxed at preferential rates: 0%, 15%, or 20%
• 2024 Long-Term 0% rate: Single up to $47,025 | MFJ up to $94,050 | HoH up to $63,000
• 2024 Long-Term 15% rate: Single $47,026–$518,900 | MFJ $94,051–$583,750
• 2024 Long-Term 20% rate: Single above $518,900 | MFJ above $583,750
• Capital Loss Deduction Limit: Maximum $3,000/year against ordinary income; excess carries forward indefinitely
Key Forms That Feed Into Schedule D
•	Form 1099-B: Proceeds from broker transactions — most common source
•	Form 8949: Sales and Other Dispositions of Capital Assets — all transactions listed here first, then totals go to Schedule D
•	Form 4797: Sales of Business Property
Wash Sale Rule — Frequently Tested
WASH SALE	If a taxpayer sells a security at a loss and repurchases the same or substantially identical security within 30 days BEFORE or AFTER the sale, the loss is DISALLOWED. The disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement shares.

SCHEDULE E — Supplemental Income and Loss
Definition: Schedule E reports income and losses from rental real estate, royalties, partnerships, S corporations, estates, and trusts.
Part I: Rental Real Estate and Royalties
•	Report rent received as gross income
•	Deductible expenses: Mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, repairs, depreciation, management fees, advertising
•	Depreciation: Residential rental property depreciated over 27.5 years (straight-line method)
Passive Activity Loss Rules — CRITICAL
PASSIVE ACTIVITY LOSS RULES (PAL)
• Rental activities are PRESUMED PASSIVE — losses generally cannot offset active income (wages, business income)
• EXCEPTION 1 (Active Participation): Taxpayers who actively participate in rental AND have AGI under $100,000 can deduct up to $25,000 of rental losses against ordinary income
• The $25,000 allowance phases out $1 for every $2 of AGI over $100,000 — fully eliminated at $150,000 AGI
• EXCEPTION 2 (Real Estate Professional): If taxpayer spends more than 750 hours/year AND more than 50% of working time in real estate activities, rental is NOT passive
• Suspended losses: Passive losses that cannot be used in current year are 'suspended' and carried forward until property is sold or there is passive income
Part II: Partnerships and S Corporations
•	Income/loss from K-1 (Form 1065 for partnerships, Schedule K-1 Form 1120-S for S corps)
•	At-risk rules: Cannot deduct losses beyond the amount you have 'at risk' (basis + loans you are personally liable for)

HSA — Health Savings Account & Form 8889
Definition: An HSA is a tax-advantaged savings account for individuals with a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) to pay for qualified medical expenses.
The Triple Tax Advantage — Most Powerful Tax Benefit Available
HSA TRIPLE TAX ADVANTAGE
• TAX ADVANTAGE 1: Contributions are tax-deductible (or pre-tax if through employer)
• TAX ADVANTAGE 2: Earnings grow tax-free inside the HSA
• TAX ADVANTAGE 3: Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free
• BONUS: After age 65, withdrawals for any purpose are taxed as ordinary income (like a Traditional IRA) — no penalty
2024 HSA Contribution Limits
•	Self-only HDHP coverage: $4,150
•	Family HDHP coverage: $8,300
•	Catch-up contribution (age 55+): Additional $1,000
HDHP Requirements (2024)
•	Minimum deductible: $1,600 (self-only) | $3,200 (family)
•	Maximum out-of-pocket: $8,050 (self-only) | $16,100 (family)
Form 8889 — Three Parts
•	Part I: HSA Contributions — reports contributions, calculates deduction (flows to Schedule 1, Line 13)
•	Part II: HSA Distributions — reports amounts withdrawn; non-medical distributions taxed + 20% penalty
•	Part III: Income and Additional Tax for Failure to Remain Eligible
Forms 8959 and 8960 — Connected to High-Income Taxpayers
FORM 8959	Additional Medicare Tax (0.9%) on wages/SE income over $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ). Employers withhold on wages over $200K but do not know about spouse's income — Form 8959 reconciles this.
FORM 8960	Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) of 3.8% on the lesser of: (a) net investment income, or (b) MAGI exceeding $200K (single) / $250K (MFJ). Investment income includes interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income, and passive activity income.

FOREIGN INCOME — Form 2555 and Form 1116
US Citizens and resident aliens are taxed on WORLDWIDE income. Two methods exist to reduce double taxation for those with foreign income.
Form 2555 — Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE)
FEIE OVERVIEW
• 2024 Exclusion Amount: Up to $126,500 of foreign earned income can be EXCLUDED from US taxable income
• ELIGIBILITY TEST 1 — Bona Fide Residence Test: Must be a bona fide resident of a foreign country for a full tax year
• ELIGIBILITY TEST 2 — Physical Presence Test: Must be physically present in a foreign country for at least 330 FULL days during any consecutive 12-month period
• Foreign Housing Exclusion/Deduction: Additional exclusion for housing costs above a base amount
• IMPORTANT: FEIE excludes income but does NOT eliminate SE tax — self-employed individuals still owe SE tax on excluded income
• Stacking Rule: Remaining income after FEIE is taxed at the rate that would apply if the excluded amount were included (beneficial bracket rates preserved)
Form 1116 — Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)
FOREIGN TAX CREDIT OVERVIEW
• Purpose: Dollar-for-dollar credit against US tax liability for income taxes paid to a foreign country
• Who Uses It: US taxpayers with foreign investment income (dividends, interest) or foreign earned income not using FEIE
• Limitation: Credit cannot exceed the US tax that would be due on the same foreign income (proportional calculation)
• Excess Credits: Unused foreign tax credits can be carried back 1 year and carried forward 10 years
• De Minimis Exception: If total foreign taxes are $300 or less (single) / $600 or less (MFJ) AND all from 1099-DIV, taxpayer can claim the credit directly on Schedule 3 without filing Form 1116
FEIE vs. Foreign Tax Credit — Which to Choose?
KEY DECISION	FEIE is generally better when: taxpayer's foreign country has a LOW tax rate OR NO tax (tax-free countries). FTC is generally better when: foreign tax rate is HIGH (close to or exceeding US rate). Cannot use FEIE for the same income you use FTC — must choose (election, revocation rules apply).

TAX CREDITS — Child, Education, Energy, Earned Income
Child Tax Credit (CTC) — Form 1040, Schedule 8812
CHILD TAX CREDIT 2024
• Amount: $2,000 per qualifying child under age 17 at end of tax year
• Refundable Portion: Additional Child Tax Credit (ACTC) — up to $1,700 per child is refundable
• Phaseout: Begins at $200,000 AGI (single) / $400,000 AGI (MFJ) — reduces by $50 per $1,000 over threshold
• Credit for Other Dependents (ODC): $500 non-refundable credit for dependents who do not qualify for CTC (age 17+ dependents, elderly parents, non-child relatives)
• QUALIFYING CHILD TEST: Must meet relationship, age, residency, support, and joint return tests
Earned Income Credit (EIC/EITC) — Most Audited Credit
EIC KEY FACTS 2024
• Who Qualifies: Low-to-moderate income workers with or without children; must have EARNED income
• Maximum Credit: $7,830 (3+ qualifying children) | $6,960 (2 children) | $4,213 (1 child) | $632 (no children)
• DISQUALIFYING FACTORS: Investment income over $11,600; no SSN; MFS filing status; foreign earned income excluded under FEIE; being a qualifying child of another
• Age Requirement (no children): Age 25–64
• Phaseout: Depends on filing status and number of children — has both phase-in and phase-out ranges
• Due Diligence: Paid preparers must complete Form 8867 (EIC Due Diligence Checklist) — $620 penalty per return for failure
American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) — Form 8863
AOTC 2024
• Amount: Up to $2,500 per eligible student (100% of first $2,000 + 25% of next $2,000 qualified expenses)
• Refundable: 40% refundable (up to $1,000) even if no tax liability
• Eligible Students: First 4 years of post-secondary education; enrolled at least half-time; no felony drug conviction
• Qualified Expenses: Tuition, fees, course materials (books, supplies, equipment REQUIRED for enrollment)
• Phaseout: $80,000–$90,000 AGI (single) | $160,000–$180,000 AGI (MFJ)
• Lifetime Limit: 4 tax years per student (first 4 years of college only)
Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC) — Form 8863
•	Amount: 20% of first $10,000 of qualified expenses = maximum $2,000 per RETURN (not per student)
•	NOT refundable — can only offset tax liability
•	No limit on number of years — available for graduate, professional, and continuing education
•	Phaseout: Same as AOTC ($80K-$90K single / $160K-$180K MFJ)
•	Cannot claim AOTC and LLC for same student in same year
Child and Dependent Care Credit — Form 2441
FORM 2441 KEY RULES
• Purpose: Credit for expenses paid to care for qualifying persons while taxpayer works or looks for work
• Qualifying Person: Child under age 13 OR dependent/spouse who is physically or mentally incapable of self-care
• Maximum Qualifying Expenses: $3,000 for one qualifying person | $6,000 for two or more
• Credit Percentage: 20%–35% depending on AGI; phases down to 20% at AGI over $43,000
• Provider Information: Must report provider's name, address, SSN or EIN on Form 2441
• Employer-Provided Benefits: Must subtract employer-provided dependent care benefits (Form W-2, Box 10) from qualifying expenses before calculating credit
• NOT refundable (except temporarily during COVID years — not applicable in 2024)
Energy Credits — Forms 5695 and 8936
RESIDENTIAL CLEAN ENERGY CREDIT (Form 5695)
• Non-business Energy Property Credit (Sec 25C): 30% credit for energy-efficient home improvements — insulation, windows, doors, heat pumps, water heaters — annual limit $1,200 ($2,000 for heat pumps)
• Residential Clean Energy Credit (Sec 25D): 30% credit for solar panels, solar water heaters, geothermal heat pumps, small wind turbines — NO annual dollar limit; any excess carries forward
• Both credits require: Primary residence in the US
CLEAN VEHICLE CREDITS (Form 8936)
• New Clean Vehicle Credit: Up to $7,500 for new EVs/PHEVs meeting battery and mineral sourcing requirements
• Used Clean Vehicle Credit: Up to $4,000 or 30% of vehicle price (lesser) for used EVs
• Income Limits: $150,000 (single) | $300,000 (MFJ) for new vehicle | $75,000 single | $150,000 MFJ for used
• Vehicle MSRP Limits: $55,000 for cars | $80,000 for SUVs, trucks, vans
• 2024 Change: Credit can now be applied at point of sale (transferred to dealer)
Saver's Credit — Form 8880
•	Credit for low/moderate income taxpayers who contribute to 401(k), IRA, SIMPLE, SEP, 403(b)
•	2024 Credit Rate: 10%, 20%, or 50% of up to $2,000 (single) / $4,000 (MFJ) of contributions
•	Maximum Credit: $1,000 single / $2,000 MFJ
•	Income Limit: $38,250 (single) / $76,500 (MFJ) for 2024 — above these, credit is zero
•	NOT available to full-time students or anyone who can be claimed as a dependent

RETIREMENT — Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, RMD
Traditional IRA
TRADITIONAL IRA 2024
• Contribution Limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+)
• Deductibility: Depends on whether taxpayer is covered by a workplace plan and AGI level
• If NOT covered by workplace plan: Contribution is fully deductible regardless of income
• If covered by workplace plan — Deductibility phases out: Single $77,000–$87,000 | MFJ $123,000–$143,000 (for covered spouse)
• Distributions: Taxable as ordinary income; 10% early withdrawal penalty if under age 59½
• Penalty Exceptions: Death, disability, first-time homebuyer ($10K lifetime), higher education expenses, health insurance premiums (unemployed), substantially equal periodic payments (SEPP)
• Reported on: Form 1040, Line 4b (taxable amount)
Roth IRA
ROTH IRA 2024
• Contribution Limit: Same as Traditional IRA ($7,000 / $8,000) — combined with Traditional IRA
• Tax Treatment: Contributions are NOT deductible (after-tax dollars go in)
• Qualified Distributions: Tax-FREE and penalty-free if: account held at least 5 years AND taxpayer is 59½+, disabled, or death
• Income Limit: Single $146,000–$161,000 | MFJ $230,000–$240,000 phaseout to make contributions
• Backdoor Roth: High-income earners who cannot directly contribute to Roth can make non-deductible Traditional IRA contribution then convert — Pro-Rata Rule applies if other Traditional IRA funds exist
• Ordering Rules for Withdrawals: Contributions first (always tax/penalty free) → Conversions → Earnings last
• No RMDs: Roth IRA owners are NOT required to take Required Minimum Distributions during their lifetime
Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)
RMD RULES 2024 (SECURE 2.0)
• RMD Start Age: Age 73 for those who turn 73 in 2023 or later (SECURE 2.0 change from SECURE Act's age 72)
• Accounts Subject to RMD: Traditional IRA, SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA, 401(k), 403(b), 457(b) — NOT Roth IRA
• Calculation: Divide prior year December 31 account balance by the applicable Life Expectancy Factor from IRS Uniform Lifetime Table
• First RMD: Can be delayed until April 1 of the year following the year you turn 73 — BUT this causes TWO RMDs in that year
• Penalty for Not Taking RMD: 25% of the amount that should have been withdrawn (reduced to 10% if corrected within 2 years)
• Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD): Taxpayers 70½+ can donate up to $105,000 directly from IRA to charity — excluded from income (better than itemizing)

SOCIAL SECURITY BENEFIT TAXATION
Social Security benefits may be partially taxable depending on the taxpayer's 'Combined Income' (a specific IRS formula).
Combined Income Formula
FORMULA	Combined Income = AGI + Non-taxable interest + 50% of Social Security benefits received
Taxation Thresholds — Must Memorize
SS BENEFIT TAXATION TIERS
• If Combined Income is BELOW $25,000 (single) / $32,000 (MFJ): 0% of SS benefits are taxable
• If Combined Income is $25,000–$34,000 (single) / $32,000–$44,000 (MFJ): Up to 50% of SS benefits may be taxable
• If Combined Income EXCEEDS $34,000 (single) / $44,000 (MFJ): Up to 85% of SS benefits may be taxable
• MAXIMUM taxable: Never more than 85% — the other 15% is always tax-free
• Calculation Worksheet: IRS Publication 915 or the worksheet in the Form 1040 instructions
• MFS Filing Status: Almost always causes 85% of SS to be taxable (threshold is $0 if spouses lived together any time during the year)

ESTIMATED TAX — Form 2210
Definition: Taxpayers who do not have sufficient withholding must pay estimated taxes quarterly to avoid underpayment penalties.
Who Must Pay Estimated Tax
•	Self-employed individuals (no employer withholding)
•	Investors with large capital gains, dividends, or interest
•	Retired taxpayers with pension/IRA income and no or insufficient withholding
•	Anyone who expects to owe $1,000 or more in federal tax after withholding
Safe Harbor Rules — Avoid Penalty
ESTIMATED TAX SAFE HARBORS
• SAFE HARBOR 1: Pay at least 90% of current year's tax liability through withholding and/or estimated payments
• SAFE HARBOR 2: Pay 100% of prior year's tax liability (if prior year AGI was $150,000 or less)
• SAFE HARBOR 3 (Higher Income): Pay 110% of prior year's tax liability if prior year AGI EXCEEDED $150,000
• Satisfying EITHER safe harbor completely eliminates the underpayment penalty
• Payment Due Dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15 (of following year)
Form 2210
•	Purpose: Calculate underpayment penalty for failing to pay enough estimated tax
•	Can be waived if: Casualty, disaster, or unusual circumstances; retired/disabled in year turning 62+ or became disabled
•	2024 Penalty Rate: Based on the federal short-term rate + 3% (changes quarterly)

FORM 2848 — Power of Attorney and Declaration of Representative
Definition: Form 2848 authorizes a designated representative (enrolled agent, CPA, attorney) to represent the taxpayer before the IRS.
What Form 2848 Authorizes
•	Receive and inspect confidential tax information
•	Represent the taxpayer in IRS meetings, hearings, and appeals
•	Sign consents and execute waivers on behalf of taxpayer (if authorized in Part I)
•	Substitute another representative
Who Can Be Listed on Form 2848 (Qualified Representatives)
•	Attorneys, CPAs, Enrolled Agents (unlimited representation rights)
•	Enrolled Retirement Plan Agents and Enrolled Actuaries (limited to their area)
•	Student attorneys/CPAs under special appearance (supervised)
CAF Number — Central Authorization File
CAF NUMBER	When a Form 2848 is filed, the IRS assigns the representative a CAF (Central Authorization File) number. The CAF is a database that allows IRS employees to verify a representative's authority without the taxpayer needing to be present. Enrolled Agents have a permanent CAF number; they use it on all future authorizations.
Difference from Form 8821
•	Form 8821 (Tax Information Authorization): Only authorizes someone to RECEIVE AND INSPECT tax info — they CANNOT represent the taxpayer or take any action on their behalf
•	Form 2848 (Power of Attorney): Authorizes representation, inspection, AND can include signing authority

 
PART 3 — TECHNICAL INTERVIEW QUESTIONS

Top 100 Technical Interview Questions
These questions are organized by topic. For each, study the Why, Strong Answer, Weak Answer, Red Flags, and Follow-up until you can answer without notes.

Section A — Form 1040 & Filing Fundamentals

Q1. Walk me through the structure of Form 1040 from top to bottom.
WHY ASKED	Tests whether you actually understand the tax formula or just filled in software fields. Senior-level question asked of all candidates.
STRONG ANSWER	Form 1040 starts with gross income — wages, interest, dividends, Schedule C, D, E income. We subtract Schedule 1 above-the-line adjustments like IRA, HSA, and SE tax deduction to arrive at AGI. From AGI we subtract either the standard deduction or Schedule A itemized deductions, whichever is greater, plus QBI deduction if applicable, to get taxable income. We apply tax brackets to get tentative tax, then subtract credits, add other taxes like SE tax and NIIT, subtract withholding and estimated payments, and arrive at refund or balance due.
WEAK ANSWER	It's the main tax form where you report your income and deductions.
RED FLAGS	Vague, no structure — signals the candidate filled in software boxes without understanding the flow. Immediate red flag for a tax preparer role.
FOLLOW-UP	What is the difference between AGI and taxable income? At what line does AGI appear? Name three above-the-line deductions.

Q2. What is AGI and why does it matter so much in tax preparation?
WHY ASKED	AGI is the gateway number that controls eligibility for dozens of deductions and credits. Interviewers want to know you understand its downstream impact.
STRONG ANSWER	AGI stands for Adjusted Gross Income. It appears on Line 11 of Form 1040. AGI is critical because it acts as the threshold for many phase-outs and floors. For example: medical deductions are only allowed to the extent they exceed 7.5% of AGI; the $25,000 rental loss allowance phases out above $100,000 AGI; education credits phase out at specific AGI levels; the EIC is limited by AGI; IRA deductibility phases out based on AGI. Reducing AGI through above-the-line deductions like HSA contributions or IRA contributions can have a multiplier effect — unlocking other deductions and credits below the line.
WEAK ANSWER	It's your income after some deductions. It determines if you can take certain deductions.
RED FLAGS	No specific examples given. Cannot explain the downstream impact — shows surface-level knowledge.
FOLLOW-UP	Name four items that are specifically limited or eliminated by a taxpayer's AGI level. What is the difference between AGI and MAGI?

Q3. A client received a W-2 with $80,000 in Box 1 and Box 12 shows Code W for $2,000. How do you handle this?
WHY ASKED	Tests practical knowledge of W-2 Box 12 codes and HSA employer contributions — a very common real-world scenario.
STRONG ANSWER	Box 1 wages of $80,000 go to Form 1040 Line 1a. Box 12 Code W represents employer HSA contributions — $2,000. These employer contributions are already excluded from Box 1 wages, so they are NOT deductible by the employee again. However, they count toward the annual HSA contribution limit. I'd enter the $2,000 on Form 8889, Part I, Line 9 as employer contributions. The client could only deduct additional HSA contributions if they contributed their own money above the employer's $2,000 up to the annual limit of $4,150 (self-only 2024). I'd also check if the plan is a qualifying HDHP.
WEAK ANSWER	I'd enter $80,000 as wages and look up what Code W means.
RED FLAGS	Did not know Code W, did not know it reduces employee deduction capacity, did not mention Form 8889.
FOLLOW-UP	What if the same client also made a $1,500 personal HSA contribution? How much can they deduct? What if they were enrolled in Medicare?

Q4. A single taxpayer has wages of $50,000 and mortgage interest of $8,000, state taxes of $6,000, and charitable donations of $3,000. Should they itemize?
WHY ASKED	Basic but crucial test of standard vs itemized decision-making that happens on EVERY return.
STRONG ANSWER	Let's calculate Schedule A total: Mortgage interest $8,000 plus state taxes $6,000 — but the SALT cap is $10,000, so all $6,000 is allowed since it is under the cap. Plus charitable donations $3,000. Schedule A total equals $17,000. The 2024 standard deduction for single is $14,600. Since $17,000 exceeds $14,600, I would recommend itemizing. The benefit of itemizing over standard deduction is $2,400. However, I'd also consider whether the client has any other itemizable expenses like medical or investment interest, and whether the complexity of Schedule A is worth $2,400 in additional tax savings at their marginal rate.
WEAK ANSWER	I'd add up the deductions and see which is bigger.
RED FLAGS	Correct conclusion but didn't mention SALT cap, didn't mention 2024 specific amounts, didn't frame the analysis professionally.
FOLLOW-UP	What if the state taxes were $12,000? What if the client is 67 years old? What effect does filing status have on this analysis?

Q5. Client files MFJ with two children under 17. AGI is $180,000. What is their Child Tax Credit?
WHY ASKED	Tests knowledge of CTC calculation including the phaseout.
STRONG ANSWER	The base CTC is $2,000 per qualifying child, so $4,000 total for two children. The phaseout for MFJ begins at $400,000 AGI. Since their AGI is $180,000, which is well below $400,000, there is NO phaseout and they receive the full $4,000 Child Tax Credit. Of that $4,000, up to $3,400 could be refundable as Additional Child Tax Credit on Schedule 8812 if they owe less than $4,000 in tax — up to $1,700 per child refundable. I'd also check if there are any other dependents who might qualify for the $500 Credit for Other Dependents.
WEAK ANSWER	They get $2,000 per child so $4,000 total.
RED FLAGS	Did not mention the phaseout threshold, did not explain ACTC refundable portion, missed Credit for Other Dependents.
FOLLOW-UP	What if their AGI was $410,000? What if one child is 17 years old? What if one child has an ITIN instead of an SSN?

Section B — Schedule C, Self-Employment & SE Tax

Q6. An Uber driver earned $42,000 in app income and drove 28,000 miles for business. How do you calculate their net Schedule C profit?
WHY ASKED	Real-world gig economy scenario — extremely common in current tax practices.
STRONG ANSWER	This is a Schedule C situation. Gross income is $42,000. The driver can deduct vehicle expenses using either the standard mileage rate or actual expenses. Using the 2024 standard mileage rate of 67 cents per mile: 28,000 miles × $0.67 = $18,760 vehicle expense deduction. I would also look for other deductible expenses: phone bills for business use percentage, supplies, any platform subscription fees, and any required business licenses. If their other deductible expenses total say $3,000, net profit would be approximately $42,000 - $18,760 - $3,000 = $20,240. This net profit flows to Schedule 1 and triggers self-employment tax. I'd calculate SE tax: $20,240 × 92.35% × 15.3% = approximately $2,862 SE tax. Then 50% of SE tax ($1,431) is deducted above the line on Schedule 1.
WEAK ANSWER	I'd put $42,000 as income and deduct the mileage.
RED FLAGS	Did not know the 2024 mileage rate, did not explain the SE tax calculation chain, did not mention the 50% SE tax deduction.
FOLLOW-UP	What if the driver also paid $1,200 in health insurance premiums? What if they want to use actual expenses instead of mileage? Can they switch methods each year?

Q7. Explain the home office deduction — who qualifies and how is it calculated?
WHY ASKED	High-frequency question for any practice with self-employed clients.
STRONG ANSWER	The home office deduction is available to self-employed individuals who use a portion of their home REGULARLY and EXCLUSIVELY for business. Employees working from home CANNOT take this deduction after TCJA 2017 — that's a common misconception. Two methods: Simplified Method — $5 per square foot of business space, maximum 300 square feet, maximum deduction of $1,500 per year, no depreciation recapture on sale. Actual Expense Method — allocate actual home expenses (mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation) based on the percentage of home used for business (office square footage divided by total home square footage). This method can give a larger deduction but requires tracking and creates depreciation recapture on home sale. The deduction cannot create a business loss — it's limited to net profit. Excess carries forward.
WEAK ANSWER	You deduct a portion of home expenses if you work from home.
RED FLAGS	Did not mention regular and exclusive use test, did not say employees cannot claim this, did not explain two methods or depreciation recapture.
FOLLOW-UP	Can an employee working from home in 2024 take a home office deduction? What is the difference between the two calculation methods in terms of long-term consequences when the home is sold?

Section C — Schedule D & Capital Gains

Q8. A client sold stock for $50,000 that they bought for $30,000 two years ago. How is this taxed?
WHY ASKED	Fundamental capital gains question — baseline competency test.
STRONG ANSWER	The client has a $20,000 capital gain ($50,000 proceeds minus $30,000 basis). Since the stock was held for more than one year, this is a LONG-TERM capital gain eligible for preferential tax rates. The rates depend on their taxable income. In 2024, if their taxable income falls in the 0% bracket (up to $47,025 single or $94,050 MFJ), the entire gain is tax-free. If in the middle bracket, it's taxed at 15%. High-income taxpayers pay 20%. I'd also check if there's any Net Investment Income Tax (Form 8960) if their MAGI exceeds $200,000 single or $250,000 MFJ — that would add 3.8% on top. I'd report this on Form 8949 first, then the totals flow to Schedule D Part II.
WEAK ANSWER	It's a long-term gain taxed at capital gains rates, probably 15%.
RED FLAGS	Did not mention Form 8949, did not know 2024 thresholds, did not mention NIIT, assumed 15% without qualifying.
FOLLOW-UP	What if there were also short-term losses of $8,000? What is Form 8949 and why is it used before Schedule D? What is the wash sale rule and could it apply here?

Q9. A client has $15,000 in capital losses and no capital gains. How much can they deduct?
WHY ASKED	Capital loss limitation — frequently misunderstood.
STRONG ANSWER	A taxpayer can deduct capital losses against capital gains first. Since there are no capital gains here, the losses cannot all be used in the current year. The IRS limits the deduction of net capital losses against ordinary income to $3,000 per year ($1,500 for MFS). So the client can deduct $3,000 in the current year against ordinary income like wages. The remaining $12,000 of capital losses carry forward to future years indefinitely and can be used against capital gains or up to $3,000 per year of ordinary income in each future year. The carryforward is tracked on Schedule D.
WEAK ANSWER	They can deduct $3,000 and lose the rest.
RED FLAGS	Did not mention carryforward, said 'lose the rest' — incorrect and alarming to an interviewer.
FOLLOW-UP	What if in the following year the client has $5,000 in capital gains? How does the carryforward interact with that? What is the order of netting short-term and long-term gains and losses?

Section D — Foreign Income, FEIE, and Foreign Tax Credit

Q10. A US citizen lives and works in Germany. Their German employer paid them €80,000 (approximately $87,000 USD). Germany withheld €24,000 in income tax. Must they file a US return? What options do they have?
WHY ASKED	Tests understanding of worldwide taxation and the two primary relief mechanisms.
STRONG ANSWER	Yes, US citizens must file a US federal tax return regardless of where they live or work — this is the worldwide income principle. They have two options to avoid double taxation: Option 1 — Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Form 2555): If they meet the bona fide residence test (living in Germany as a resident for a full tax year) or the physical presence test (330 days in a 12-month period), they can exclude up to $126,500 of earned income from US taxation in 2024. Option 2 — Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116): They can claim a dollar-for-dollar credit for taxes paid to Germany, up to the US tax that would have been due on that income. Since Germany's tax rate appears high relative to the US rate, the Foreign Tax Credit would likely eliminate most or all US tax. Excess FTC can be carried back 1 year or forward 10 years. They cannot use FEIE and FTC on the same income — must choose. FBAR (FinCEN 114) filing may also be required if foreign bank account balance exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year.
WEAK ANSWER	They can exclude foreign income so they don't pay US tax.
RED FLAGS	Only mentioned FEIE, did not mention FTC as an alternative, did not explain when each is better, forgot FBAR.
FOLLOW-UP	Which method would you recommend in this case and why? What is FBAR and what is the penalty for failing to file? What happens if the taxpayer uses FEIE but later wants to revoke the election?

Section E — Schedule E & Rental Income

Q11. A client has a rental property with $24,000 gross rent, $8,000 mortgage interest, $4,000 property taxes, $3,000 repairs, and $9,000 depreciation. Their AGI before rental is $90,000. Calculate their rental income and advise on deductibility.
WHY ASKED	Complex Schedule E question combining rental math with passive activity rules.
STRONG ANSWER	Schedule E calculation: Gross rent $24,000 minus deductible expenses: mortgage interest $8,000, property taxes $4,000, repairs $3,000, depreciation $9,000. Total expenses = $24,000. Net rental income or loss = $24,000 - $24,000 = zero. If expenses exceeded income and created a loss, we'd need to analyze the passive activity rules. But in this case, the rental breaks even. Regarding the $9,000 depreciation: residential rental property is depreciated over 27.5 years on a straight-line basis. The client should provide the original purchase price, date of purchase, and land value so I can verify the annual depreciation is correct. I'd also note that depreciation reduces the tax basis of the property and creates depreciation recapture (taxed at 25% rate) when the property is eventually sold.
WEAK ANSWER	I'd subtract the expenses from the rent and report the net on Schedule E.
RED FLAGS	Did not mention depreciation recapture, did not calculate correctly, did not mention passive activity rules.
FOLLOW-UP	What if the expenses were $30,000 creating a $6,000 loss? Would the client be able to deduct that loss? What if their AGI was $130,000?

Section F — HSA, Form 8889, and High-Income Medicare Taxes

Q12. Explain the difference between an HSA, FSA, and HRA. When can each be used?
WHY ASKED	Interviewers want to know you can distinguish between similar-sounding benefit accounts that clients confuse constantly.
STRONG ANSWER	HSA (Health Savings Account): Requires enrollment in a High-Deductible Health Plan. Contributions are triple tax-advantaged — deductible going in, grow tax-free, and tax-free coming out for qualified medical expenses. Funds roll over year to year indefinitely. The account belongs to the employee and travels with them between employers. 2024 limits: $4,150 self-only, $8,300 family. FSA (Flexible Spending Account): No HDHP requirement. Use-it-or-lose-it — funds generally must be used in the plan year (employers may allow $640 carryover or 2.5-month grace period in 2024). Funded pre-tax through payroll deduction. Limited FSA can coexist with HSA. HRA (Health Reimbursement Arrangement): Funded ONLY by the employer — employees cannot contribute. Reimburses qualified medical expenses tax-free. Rules vary significantly by HRA type (ICHRA, QSEHRA, standard HRA). Funds may or may not roll over depending on employer plan design.
WEAK ANSWER	HSA is for high-deductible plans. FSA you lose at the end of the year.
RED FLAGS	Did not explain HRA, did not mention 2024 limits, did not explain rollover rules precisely, missed that FSA is use-it-or-lose-it for most but has a grace period option.
FOLLOW-UP	Can a taxpayer contribute to both an HSA and an FSA in the same year? What is a Limited Purpose FSA and how does it interact with an HSA?

Section G — IRA, Roth IRA, and RMDs

Q13. A 30-year-old client wants to contribute to a Roth IRA but their AGI is $200,000 (single). Can they?
WHY ASKED	Tests knowledge of Roth IRA income limits and backdoor Roth strategy.
STRONG ANSWER	A single taxpayer cannot make a direct Roth IRA contribution in 2024 if their AGI exceeds $161,000 — the phaseout ends at $161,000. At $200,000 AGI, a direct Roth IRA contribution is completely phased out. However, they can use the Backdoor Roth strategy: Step 1 — Make a non-deductible Traditional IRA contribution of up to $7,000 (no income limit applies to Traditional IRA contributions, only to deductibility). Step 2 — Convert the Traditional IRA to a Roth IRA shortly after. If the Traditional IRA contains ONLY this new non-deductible contribution and no other pre-tax funds, the conversion is essentially tax-free (taxable amount is zero since basis equals the conversion amount). They must file Form 8606 to track the non-deductible basis. Important warning: If the client has other Traditional IRA funds, the Pro-Rata Rule applies and part of the conversion will be taxable.
WEAK ANSWER	Yes, they can contribute to a Roth IRA. Everyone can contribute.
RED FLAGS	Completely wrong on the income limit — disqualifying error for any tax preparer position.
FOLLOW-UP	What is the Pro-Rata Rule and how does it affect the Backdoor Roth strategy? What is Form 8606 and when must it be filed?

Q14. A client turns 73 in 2024 and has a Traditional IRA worth $500,000 at December 31, 2023. Calculate their RMD.
WHY ASKED	Direct calculation question — tests whether you can actually apply the RMD rules.
STRONG ANSWER	Under SECURE 2.0, the RMD starting age for someone who turns 73 in 2024 is age 73. The calculation uses the prior year-end account balance divided by the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table distribution period. For a 73-year-old in 2024, the Uniform Lifetime Table factor is 26.5 years. RMD = $500,000 ÷ 26.5 = approximately $18,868. This amount is taxable as ordinary income on Form 1040 Line 4b and reported to the taxpayer on Form 1099-R with a distribution code. Since this is their first RMD year (turning 73 in 2024), they have the option to delay it until April 1, 2025. However, delaying means they will have TWO RMDs in 2025 — one for 2024 (the delayed first one due by April 1) and one for 2025 (due by December 31). I would typically advise against delaying because two RMDs in one year increases taxable income significantly and can trigger higher SS benefit taxation or push them into a higher bracket.
WEAK ANSWER	Divide the account balance by a life expectancy factor from the IRS table.
RED FLAGS	Correct concept but could not name the table, did not know the factor for age 73, did not mention the April 1 delay option or its implications.
FOLLOW-UP	What is the penalty for missing an RMD? What is a Qualified Charitable Distribution and how does it interact with the RMD? If the client has two IRAs, how is the RMD calculated?

Section H — Social Security Benefits Taxation

Q15. A retired couple filing MFJ received $30,000 in Social Security benefits. Their other income is $40,000 in pension income and $4,000 in tax-exempt municipal bond interest. How much of their SS is taxable?
WHY ASKED	Tests whether you can apply the combined income formula — very common in practices with retired clients.
STRONG ANSWER	I need to calculate their Combined Income. The formula is: AGI plus non-taxable interest plus 50% of SS benefits. Their AGI = $40,000 pension income. Non-taxable interest = $4,000 (municipal bond interest — important to note this IS included in the combined income formula even though it's tax-exempt). 50% of SS = 50% × $30,000 = $15,000. Combined Income = $40,000 + $4,000 + $15,000 = $59,000. For MFJ: the $32,000–$44,000 bracket means up to 50% is taxable; above $44,000, up to 85% is taxable. Since $59,000 exceeds $44,000, up to 85% of Social Security benefits may be taxable. Maximum taxable amount = 85% × $30,000 = $25,500. I'd use the SS Benefits Worksheet in the Form 1040 instructions to calculate the exact taxable amount, which will be reported on Line 6b.
WEAK ANSWER	Since they made over $44,000, they have to pay tax on 85% of their Social Security.
RED FLAGS	Said 85% is automatically taxable — incorrect. It's UP TO 85% determined by the worksheet. Also, simply saying 85% without applying the formula shows memorization without understanding.
FOLLOW-UP	Why is tax-exempt municipal bond interest included in the combined income formula? What planning strategies can reduce the taxable portion of Social Security benefits?

Section I — Earned Income Credit (EIC) & Due Diligence

Q16. Name three reasons a taxpayer might be disqualified from claiming the EIC.
WHY ASKED	EIC due diligence is critically important — preparers face $620 penalties per return for failure to exercise due diligence.
STRONG ANSWER	There are several EIC disqualifiers. Three common ones: First, investment income exceeding $11,600 in 2024 — if a taxpayer has more than this in investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income, passive income), they are completely ineligible for EIC regardless of earned income. Second, using Married Filing Separately filing status — MFS taxpayers are categorically disqualified from EIC. Third, being a qualifying child of another taxpayer — someone who can be claimed as a dependent on another return cannot claim EIC themselves, even if they have earned income. Additional disqualifiers include: no Social Security Number (must have SSN valid for employment), using Form 2555 to exclude foreign earned income, and claiming EIC for a child who fails the qualifying child tests.
WEAK ANSWER	They earn too much money or don't have any kids.
RED FLAGS	Too vague — 'earn too much' without specifying the investment income limit vs earned income limit distinction is a red flag. Missing specific disqualifiers shows lack of preparation.
FOLLOW-UP	What is Form 8867 and when must it be completed? What are the four due diligence requirements for paid preparers claiming EIC? What are the penalties for EIC due diligence failures?

Section J — Estimated Tax & Form 2210

Q17. A freelance designer had no withholding and owes $15,000 in federal tax for 2024. Their 2023 tax liability was $12,000 and 2023 AGI was $120,000. Will they owe an underpayment penalty?
WHY ASKED	Tests safe harbor knowledge — fundamental for any self-employed client work.
STRONG ANSWER	Let me analyze both safe harbors. Safe Harbor 1: Pay 90% of 2024 current year tax. 90% of $15,000 = $13,500 required. If they paid at least $13,500 in estimated payments, no penalty. Safe Harbor 2: Pay 100% of prior year tax if prior year AGI ≤ $150,000. Their 2023 AGI was $120,000 (under $150,000), so the 100% safe harbor applies. They need to have paid at least $12,000 (their 2023 liability) in 2024 estimated payments. If they paid at least $12,000, Safe Harbor 2 is met even though they owe $15,000 in 2024 tax. I would check their estimated payment records. If neither safe harbor was met, Form 2210 calculates the penalty. I'd also advise them going forward to make quarterly estimated payments by April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15 to avoid this situation in 2025.
WEAK ANSWER	They'd owe an underpayment penalty if they didn't pay enough.
RED FLAGS	Correct in concept but did not apply the actual safe harbor rules or numbers — does not demonstrate working knowledge.
FOLLOW-UP	What if their 2023 AGI was $175,000? Which safe harbor would apply then and why? What is the annualized income installment method?

Section K — IRS Representation & Form 2848

Q18. A client received an IRS CP2000 notice proposing additional tax. What is your process for handling this?
WHY ASKED	Tests practical IRS representation knowledge — Form 2848, CP notice response, and professional process.
STRONG ANSWER	A CP2000 is an Automated Underreporter (AUR) notice proposing changes because information on the return does not match IRS records from third-party sources like W-2s, 1099s, or broker statements. My process: First, obtain a Form 2848 Power of Attorney from the client authorizing me to represent them before the IRS. Submit it to the IRS CAF unit or fax it with the response. Then, review the CP2000 carefully — it lists what the IRS says was unreported and the proposed additional tax. Request the client's records for the items in question. Compare to the filed return. Three possible outcomes: (1) Client agrees — complete and sign the response agreeing to the change; (2) Client partially agrees — send a check for agreed amount and dispute the rest; (3) Client disagrees — write a response explaining why the IRS is incorrect with supporting documentation. The response deadline is typically 60 days. If more time is needed, call the IRS or send a written extension request. A CP2000 is NOT a bill and NOT an audit — it is a proposal. Responding timely prevents it from becoming an assessment.
WEAK ANSWER	I'd help them respond to the IRS and figure out if they owe more money.
RED FLAGS	No mention of Form 2848, no specific process, no mention of what CP2000 actually is, no timeline mentioned.
FOLLOW-UP	What is the difference between a CP2000 and an audit notice? What if the client ignores the CP2000? What is the Taxpayer Advocate Service and when would you use it?

Q19. What are the three levels of practice before the IRS, and which practitioners can appear at each level?
WHY ASKED	EA Part 3 knowledge test — directly relevant to your passed exam.
STRONG ANSWER	The IRS distinguishes three levels of practice before the IRS: Level 1 — Unlimited Representation Rights: Enrolled Agents, CPAs, and attorneys can represent taxpayers before ALL IRS offices including audits, collections, and appeals. They can represent taxpayers even for returns they did not prepare. Level 2 — Limited Representation Rights: Annual Filing Season Program (AFSP) participants can represent taxpayers before revenue agents, customer service representatives, and the Taxpayer Advocate in examinations of returns they prepared and signed. They cannot appear before Appeals or Collections. Level 3 — No Representation Rights: Unenrolled preparers who do not participate in AFSP generally cannot represent taxpayers before the IRS at all — they can only prepare returns. An important distinction: ANY return preparer can represent a taxpayer before the IRS Taxpayer Assistance Center (TAC) for returns they prepared and signed, but only to accompany the taxpayer — not as a representative.
WEAK ANSWER	Enrolled Agents and CPAs can represent clients. Others have limited rights.
RED FLAGS	Incomplete — did not explain the three levels clearly or name which offices each can appear before.
FOLLOW-UP	If you pass all three EA exam parts, what additional ability do you have compared to a CPA who has not passed the EA exam? What is Circular 230?

Q20. A client wants to know if the IRS can waive an underpayment penalty. What are the grounds for abatement?
WHY ASKED	Tests knowledge of IRS penalty relief — common EA representation question.
STRONG ANSWER	Yes, the IRS can abate penalties in several situations. First Abatement (FTA): For taxpayers with no penalties in the prior three years who have filed all required returns and paid or arranged to pay all taxes, the IRS will abate first-time penalties on request. This covers failure-to-file, failure-to-pay, and failure-to-deposit penalties. Reasonable Cause: If the taxpayer can demonstrate they exercised ordinary business care and prudence but were unable to comply — such as serious illness, natural disaster, or relying on incorrect professional advice — penalties may be abated. Statutory Exception: Certain penalty waivers built into the tax code, such as the underpayment penalty safe harbors on Form 2210. Correction of IRS Error: If the penalty resulted from IRS incorrect advice (written IRS guidance), the IRS must abate. Disaster Relief: IRS automatically extends deadlines and abates penalties for federally declared disaster areas.
WEAK ANSWER	You can call the IRS and ask for a waiver if you have a good reason.
RED FLAGS	No specific grounds, no mention of First Abatement (the most powerful and commonly overlooked option), no professional framing.
FOLLOW-UP	What is the specific process for requesting First Time Abatement? Can FTA be granted over the phone? What if the client received incorrect advice from the IRS that caused the penalty?

Additional Technical Questions 21–100 — Study Format
The following questions are presented with key answer points for efficient study. Practice giving full verbal answers before your interview.

Q21	What is the QBI deduction (Section 199A) and who qualifies?
Up to 20% deduction on qualified business income from pass-through entities. Phases out for service businesses (SSTB) above $191,950 single/$383,900 MFJ. Non-SSTB businesses face W-2 wage/property limitations above the threshold. Form 8995 or 8995-A.

Q22	What is the difference between a tax credit and a tax deduction?
A deduction reduces TAXABLE INCOME (value depends on tax bracket). A credit directly reduces TAX LIABILITY dollar-for-dollar. A $1,000 credit is always worth $1,000. A $1,000 deduction at 22% bracket saves $220.

Q23	What is AMT (Alternative Minimum Tax)?
A parallel tax system designed to ensure high-income taxpayers pay minimum tax. Add back certain deductions (especially SALT, ISO spreads, depreciation differences) to income, subtract AMT exemption, apply 26%/28% rate. Pay whichever is higher — regular tax or AMT. Form 6251.

Q24	Explain the filing status requirements for Head of Household.
Must be unmarried (or considered unmarried) at year-end, paid more than half the cost of maintaining a home, AND a qualifying person lived in the home more than half the year. The qualifying person for HoH is different from qualifying child for CTC.

Q25	A dependent child earned $5,000 in wages and $3,000 in investment income. Must they file?
The dependent must file because investment income exceeds $1,300 (2024 threshold). Also, investment income over $2,500 triggers the Kiddie Tax (unearned income of dependents under 19, or under 24 if full-time student, taxed at parents' rate). Form 8615.

Q26	What is a 1099-NEC and how is it reported?
Reports non-employee compensation (freelance/contract work). Goes to Schedule C as business income. Triggers self-employment tax. Box 1 amount; 1099-NEC replaced 1099-MISC Box 7 for independent contractor payments starting 2020.

Q27	What is the difference between a 1099-MISC and 1099-NEC?
1099-NEC: Non-employee compensation (independent contractors). 1099-MISC: Other income — rents (Box 1), royalties (Box 2), prizes/awards (Box 3), medical payments (Box 6). Rents and royalties go to Schedule E, not Schedule C.

Q28	How is Social Security income different from SSI for tax purposes?
Social Security retirement/disability (SSA-1099): May be partially taxable per combined income rules. Supplemental Security Income (SSI): NOT taxable — does not appear on a tax return at all. Important distinction many clients confuse.

Q29	What records should a client keep for charitable donation deductions?
Cash under $250: dated receipt or bank record. Cash $250+: written acknowledgment from charity stating no goods/services received. Non-cash $250+: written acknowledgment. Non-cash over $500: Form 8283. Non-cash over $5,000: qualified appraisal.

Q30	What is the educator expense deduction and what can it cover?
Up to $300 ($600 MFJ if both teachers) for K-12 teachers, counselors, principals for classroom supplies, books, supplementary materials, health and protective equipment. Available above-the-line on Schedule 1. Professional development and computer equipment also qualify.

Q31	What is the student loan interest deduction?
Up to $2,500 deduction on qualified student loan interest paid. Above-the-line deduction (Schedule 1). Phases out $75,000–$90,000 AGI single / $155,000–$185,000 MFJ. Cannot be claimed if filing MFS or if claimed as a dependent.

Q32	What triggers the 10% early withdrawal penalty from an IRA?
Distribution before age 59½ triggers 10% penalty plus ordinary income tax. Exceptions: death, disability, SEPP (72(t) payments), first home purchase ($10K lifetime), higher education, health insurance if unemployed 12+ weeks, medical expenses exceeding 7.5% AGI, IRS levy, military reservist distributions.

Q33	What is a Roth IRA conversion and what are the tax consequences?
Moving funds from Traditional IRA to Roth IRA. The converted amount is taxable as ordinary income in the year of conversion (no 10% penalty for the conversion itself, but 10% penalty applies if converted funds withdrawn within 5 years). Makes sense when current-year tax rate is lower than expected future rate.

Q34	What is Form 1099-R and what distribution codes are most important?
Reports distributions from IRAs, pensions, annuities, insurance contracts. Code 1: early distribution, no exception (subject to 10% penalty). Code 2: early distribution, exception applies. Code 3: disability. Code 4: death. Code 7: normal distribution (59½+). Code G: direct rollover to another qualified plan.

Q35	What is a direct rollover vs indirect rollover?
Direct rollover: trustee-to-trustee transfer; never touches taxpayer's hands; no withholding; no 60-day rule; no tax. Indirect rollover: check sent to taxpayer; 20% mandatory withholding on qualified plan distributions; taxpayer must deposit 100% (including the withheld 20% from own funds) within 60 days to avoid tax and penalty. Only one IRA-to-IRA indirect rollover allowed per 12-month period.

Q36	What is the difference between tax avoidance and tax evasion?
Tax avoidance: Legal reduction of tax liability through legitimate deductions, credits, and planning strategies. Entirely legal and encouraged. Tax evasion: Illegal failure to pay taxes — concealing income, falsifying records, claiming fraudulent deductions. Criminal offense. Never confuse these in an interview.

Q37	A client gave their adult child $30,000 as a gift in 2024. What are the gift tax implications?
The annual gift tax exclusion is $18,000 per recipient per year (2024). First $18,000 is excluded. The remaining $12,000 requires filing Form 709 (Gift Tax Return). No tax is owed currently because it reduces the lifetime exemption ($13.61 million in 2024). Gift tax returns are informational for most taxpayers.

Q38	What is the basis of inherited property?
Inherited property receives a STEPPED-UP BASIS to the fair market value as of the decedent's date of death (or alternate valuation date 6 months later if estate is large enough). This eliminates capital gain on all appreciation during the decedent's lifetime. Any subsequent sale is compared to the stepped-up FMV basis — holding period is automatically LONG-TERM regardless of when the heir sells.

Q39	What is the Kiddie Tax and when does it apply?
Unearned income (investment income) of a dependent child is taxed at the parents' marginal tax rate when the child's investment income exceeds $2,500 (2024). Applies to: children under 18, full-time students age 19-23, and anyone under 19 with earned income less than half of support. Form 8615. Purpose: prevent parents from shifting investment income to children in lower brackets.

Q40	A taxpayer contributed $5,000 to an IRA but is not eligible to deduct it. What must they do?
File Form 8606 (Nondeductible IRAs) to establish basis in the IRA. This is critical — failure to track nondeductible contributions means double taxation when funds are distributed. Form 8606 must be filed for every year a nondeductible contribution is made and must continue to track cumulative basis until all funds are withdrawn.

Q41	What is the Premium Tax Credit (PTC)?
Refundable credit to help eligible individuals and families afford health insurance purchased through the Marketplace (HealthCare.gov). Eligibility based on household income between 100%–400% of federal poverty level. Calculated on Form 8962. Advance payments (APTC) paid directly to insurer must be reconciled at filing.

Q42	What is Form 8962 and what happens if APTC exceeds PTC?
Form 8962 reconciles the Advance Premium Tax Credit with the actual credit based on final AGI. If AGI is higher than estimated when enrollment began, APTC was too large — the excess must be repaid (subject to repayment caps based on income). If AGI is lower, additional credit is received as a refund.

Q43	What does it mean for a return to be filed MFS (Married Filing Separately)?
Married Filing Separately generally results in the worst tax outcome — higher rates, many credits disallowed (EIC, childcare, education credits, PTC), 85% SS taxability if lived with spouse, reduced IRA deduction, halved SALT cap. Used in limited circumstances: protecting one spouse from other's tax liability, or when one spouse has significant medical expenses as percentage of their individual AGI.

Q44	What is the difference between a qualifying child and a qualifying relative for dependency purposes?
Qualifying Child: Must meet relationship test, age test (under 19, or under 24 if full-time student, or any age if permanently disabled), residency test (live with taxpayer more than half the year), and support test (child did not provide over half their own support). Qualifying Relative: Does NOT need to live with taxpayer (except members of household). Must meet relationship OR member of household test, gross income test (under $5,050 in 2024), support test (taxpayer provides over half), and not be a qualifying child of anyone.

Q45	What are the tiebreaker rules for the qualifying child when two taxpayers could claim the same child?
Priority order when two taxpayers both have a qualifying relationship: 1) Parent wins over non-parent; 2) If both parents, the parent with whom the child lived the most; 3) If equal time, the parent with higher AGI; 4) If neither is a parent, the taxpayer with higher AGI wins.

Q46	What is a Section 1031 like-kind exchange?
Allows deferral of capital gain tax when business or investment property is exchanged for similar property. Requirements: must involve real property (after 2017 — personal property no longer qualifies), must be like-kind (any real property for any real property), must use a qualified intermediary, must identify replacement property within 45 days and close within 180 days. Tax is deferred, not eliminated.

Q47	What is depreciation recapture on rental property?
When rental real estate is sold at a gain, the portion of the gain attributable to prior depreciation deductions (straight-line) is taxed at a maximum rate of 25% (not the 0%/15%/20% long-term capital gain rates). This is called Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain. The remaining gain above original cost basis is taxed at regular long-term capital gain rates.

Q48	How is a home sale exclusion calculated?
Under Section 121, taxpayers can exclude up to $250,000 (single) / $500,000 (MFJ) of gain on the sale of a principal residence. Requirements: owned the home for at least 2 years in the 5 years before sale AND used it as main home for at least 2 of the 5 years (use test). The 2-year periods do not need to be continuous. Partial exclusion available if sold due to job change, health reasons, or unforeseen circumstances.

Q49	What is a Qualified Opportunity Zone investment?
Investment in a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) in a designated low-income area. Benefits: deferral of capital gain reinvested within 180 days, and if held for 10+ years, gain on the QOF investment itself is permanently excluded. Promoted as way to reduce capital gains tax while investing in underserved communities.

Q50	What is the Affordable Care Act (ACA) individual mandate?
The individual mandate required most Americans to have minimum essential health coverage or pay a tax penalty. The federal penalty was reduced to $0 starting in 2019 — effectively eliminating the federal penalty. However, some states (California, Massachusetts, New Jersey, etc.) still have their own individual mandates with state penalties. The ACA marketplace subsidy (PTC) still exists and requires Form 8962.

Q51	What is Section 179 expensing?
Allows immediate deduction of business assets (machinery, equipment, vehicles, computers) in year placed in service. 2024 limit: $1,220,000. Phased out dollar-for-dollar when total property placed in service exceeds $3,050,000. Cannot create a loss — limited to business taxable income.

Q52	What is bonus depreciation?
First-year deduction for new and used property. 2024 rate is 60% (phasing down from 100% after 2022 under TCJA). Can create a loss (unlike Section 179). Applies to most business property with a recovery period of 20 years or less.

Q53	What is the difference between repairs and improvements for rental property?
Repairs (routine maintenance, fixing broken items) are currently deductible on Schedule E. Improvements (betterments, restorations, adaptations to new use) must be capitalized and depreciated. Tangible property regulations (TPRs) govern the distinction.

Q54	How do you handle a client who received a Form 1099-K from PayPal?
1099-K reports payment card and third-party network transactions. New 2024 threshold discussion: $5,000 (transitional relief). For business income (freelancer, eBay seller), report on Schedule C. For personal sales, it may be excluded or reported on Schedule D if property sold at a gain. Cannot simply ignore a 1099-K.

Q55	What is the net operating loss (NOL) carryforward?
When business deductions exceed income, creating a negative taxable income. Post-TCJA: NOLs can be carried forward indefinitely but can only offset 80% of taxable income in any given year. NOL carrybacks (2 years) were eliminated after TCJA except for farmers.

Q56	What is Form W-4 and how does it affect tax preparation?
Employee's withholding certificate — tells employer how much federal income tax to withhold. Updated version after TCJA eliminates allowances system. Employees now enter expected deductions, credits, and multiple jobs directly. Under-withholding results in a tax bill and potential underpayment penalty.

Q57	What is a qualified plan loan and how is it taxed?
Participants can borrow from 401(k) — up to $50,000 or 50% of vested balance, whichever is less. Must repay within 5 years (longer for home purchase). Failure to repay is treated as a distribution — taxable plus 10% penalty. CARES Act extended loan limits and repayment periods temporarily.

Q58	What is Form 4868 and when is it due?
Application for Automatic Extension of Time to File. Extends filing deadline 6 months (to October 15 for calendar year filers). DOES NOT extend time to PAY. Tax must be estimated and paid by April 15 to avoid failure-to-pay penalty. Extension is automatic upon filing — no IRS approval needed.

Q59	What are the civil tax penalties a preparer can face?
Under IRC Sec 6694: $1,000 penalty (or 50% of fee) for unreasonable positions not disclosed. $5,000 penalty (or 75% of fee) for reckless or intentional disregard of rules. Under Sec 6695: $50 per failure up to $27,000 per year for failure to sign, provide copy, retain records. $620 per return for EIC due diligence failure.

Q60	What is Circular 230?
Treasury Regulations (31 CFR Part 10) that govern practice of tax professionals before the IRS. Covers standards for advice, conflicts of interest, client fees (no unconscionable fees, contingent fees generally prohibited for tax returns), and disciplinary procedures. EA exam Part 3 is extensively tested on Circular 230.

Q61	What is a Coverdell Education Savings Account vs 529 plan?
Coverdell ESA: $2,000/year contribution limit, income limit ($110K single/$220K MFJ), funds must be used by age 30, can be used for K-12 AND college expenses. 529 Plan: No income limit, no contribution dollar limit (gift tax annual exclusion applies), only funds for college/vocational school are federally tax-free (though 2022 law allows up to $10K for K-12).

Q62	What is the first-year election for a new business on Schedule C?
New businesses can elect to deduct up to $5,000 of startup costs and $5,000 of organizational costs in the first year. Remaining costs are amortized over 180 months. Phases out dollar-for-dollar when costs exceed $50,000. Without the election, all startup costs are amortized.

Q63	A client received a 1099-DIV with amounts in Box 1a, 1b, and 2a. Explain each.
Box 1a: Total ordinary dividends — taxed as ordinary income. Box 1b: Qualified dividends (a subset of 1a) — taxed at preferential long-term capital gain rates (0%/15%/20%). Box 2a: Total capital gain distributions from mutual funds — taxed as long-term capital gains regardless of how long the fund was held.

Q64	What is the FBAR and who must file it?
FinCEN Form 114 (Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts). Filed by US persons who have a financial interest in or signature authority over foreign financial accounts exceeding $10,000 in aggregate value at ANY point during the calendar year. Filed electronically with FinCEN (not IRS). Due April 15 with automatic 6-month extension to October 15. Penalties: up to $10,000 per non-willful violation; criminal penalties for willful failure.

Q65	What is FATCA and Form 8938?
Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act. Form 8938 (Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets) filed WITH the tax return. Thresholds: $50,000 year-end OR $75,000 any time (single); $100,000/$150,000 (MFJ); higher thresholds for US persons abroad. FBAR and Form 8938 have different thresholds and are NOT mutually exclusive.

Q66	What is the Statute of Limitations on IRS assessments?
Standard: 3 years from the later of return due date or actual filing date. Extended to 6 years if taxpayer omits more than 25% of gross income. No limitation for fraudulent returns or failure to file. The IRS can audit any year if fraud is suspected — no time limit.

Q67	What is the difference between a tax return transcript and a tax account transcript?
Tax Return Transcript: Shows most line items from the original filed return — used for mortgage applications, financial aid. Tax Account Transcript: Shows changes to the return after filing — assessed tax, payments made, penalties, credits. Wage & Income Transcript: Shows third-party data (W-2s, 1099s) reported to IRS. Available through IRS.gov or Form 4506-C.

Q68	What is the earned income definition for HSA and IRA purposes?
Earned income for IRA contribution eligibility = wages, salaries, tips, commissions, Schedule C income, and taxable alimony received (pre-2019 divorce). Does NOT include pension, rental income, Social Security, or investment income. A taxpayer must have earned income at least equal to the IRA contribution they make.

Q69	What is Form SS-4 and when is it used in tax preparation?
Application for Employer Identification Number (EIN). Filed when a new business is formed, a trust or estate needs an EIN, or an organization needs an EIN for payroll. EINs are required for all entities with employees, partnerships, corporations, trusts, and estates. Can be applied online at IRS.gov instantly.

Q70	A client sold their primary residence after owning and living in it for 18 months. Can they exclude the gain?
Standard exclusion requires 2 years of ownership AND use. At 18 months, they do not qualify for the FULL exclusion. However, they may qualify for a PARTIAL exclusion if the sale was due to a change in employment (new job more than 50 miles from home), health reasons, or unforeseen circumstances. The partial exclusion is prorated: 18/24 months × $250,000 = $187,500 (single). If neither exception applies, the gain is fully taxable — short-term if owned less than 1 year, long-term if 12-18 months.

Q71	What is Form 8879?
IRS e-File Signature Authorization. Taxpayer authorizes an ERO (Electronic Return Originator) to transmit the return electronically using the taxpayer's PIN as a signature. Must be signed BEFORE the return is transmitted. Retained by the preparer for 3 years — not sent to IRS.

Q72	What is an ERO and EFIN?
ERO: Electronic Return Originator — a tax professional authorized to originate the electronic submission of returns to the IRS. EFIN: Electronic Filing Identification Number — the number the IRS assigns to an ERO. Required to e-file. EFINs are assigned at the firm level, not individual preparer level. Preparers use Form 8633 to apply for an EFIN.

Q73	What is PTIN and who needs one?
Preparer Tax Identification Number. Required for ANY tax professional who prepares or substantially helps prepare a federal tax return for compensation. Must be renewed annually. Applied for and renewed at IRS.gov/PTIN. Replaces SSN on tax returns to protect preparer's identity. No fee (as of 2022 court ruling).

Q74	What is Section 199A Qualified Business Income deduction and when does it NOT apply?
Section 199A allows up to 20% deduction on QBI from sole proprietorships, partnerships, S corps, and trusts. DOES NOT apply: to employees (W-2 income), C corporations, capital gains, dividends, and interest income. For Specified Service Trade or Businesses (SSTBs) like law, accounting, consulting, financial services, health — the deduction phases out completely above income thresholds ($191,950 single/$383,900 MFJ in 2024).

Q75	What is the difference between wages reported on Form W-2 Box 1 and Box 3?
Box 1: Federal taxable wages — after pre-tax deductions like 401(k), HSA, health insurance premiums. Box 3: Social Security wages — NOT reduced by 401(k) or most retirement plan contributions (Social Security tax is due on pre-retirement-contribution wages). Box 3 may be larger than Box 1. Box 3 is capped at the Social Security wage base ($168,600 for 2024).

Q76	What is imputed income and where is it reported?
Imputed income is the value of benefits that must be treated as income. Common examples: employer-provided life insurance over $50,000 (excess value reported in Box 12 Code C of W-2), personal use of a company car (Box 14 or Box 1), adoption assistance exceeding the exclusion. Increases Box 1 taxable wages.

Q77	How do you handle cryptocurrency transactions on a tax return?
Virtual currency is property (IRS Notice 2014-21). Each sale, exchange, or use to pay for goods is a taxable event. Calculate gain/loss = proceeds minus cost basis. Short vs long-term holding period determines rate. Received as payment for services = ordinary income at fair market value on date received. Mining income = ordinary income. Form 8949 for each transaction. May also require Form 1099-DA starting 2025.

Q78	What is the difference between a C corporation and S corporation for tax purposes?
C Corp: Separate taxable entity. Pays corporate income tax at 21% flat rate. Dividends paid to shareholders are taxed again at shareholder level (double taxation). No income limits. S Corp: Pass-through entity — income flows to shareholders via K-1 and is taxed on individual returns (no corporate-level tax). Limits: max 100 shareholders, one class of stock, only US citizens/residents. Shareholders who work must receive reasonable compensation (W-2 wage) to prevent SE tax avoidance.

Q79	What is the QCD and how does it save taxes compared to a regular charitable deduction?
Qualified Charitable Distribution: Taxpayers age 70½+ can donate up to $105,000 directly from their IRA to a qualified charity. The distribution is excluded from income (never included in AGI) — this is better than itemizing because: (1) most taxpayers cannot itemize (standard deduction is high), (2) even itemizers benefit because the QCD reduces AGI which can lower SS taxability, Medicare premiums (IRMAA), and other AGI-sensitive items. The QCD also counts toward the RMD requirement.

Q80	What is the difference between the tax home and the foreign tax home concepts?
Tax home for domestic purposes (travel deductions): The taxpayer's main place of business/employment, regardless of where their personal residence is. For foreign earned income exclusion purposes: Tax home must be in a foreign country — a US taxpayer maintaining an 'abode' in the US cannot claim FEIE even if working abroad extensively.

Q81	What is the penalty for failure to file vs failure to pay?
Failure to File: 5% of unpaid tax per month (or partial month), maximum 25%. Failure to Pay: 0.5% of unpaid tax per month, maximum 25%. If BOTH apply in the same month, failure-to-file rate is reduced by the failure-to-pay rate, so net is 4.5% + 0.5% = 5% max combined per month. File even if you cannot pay — the failure-to-file penalty is 10× more expensive than the failure-to-pay penalty.

Q82	What is an installment agreement and when can a taxpayer apply?
IRS payment plan for taxpayers who cannot pay full tax liability. Guaranteed Installment Agreement: For tax debt under $10,000, pay within 3 years, filed all returns, had no prior installment agreement in past 5 years — IRS must approve. Streamlined Installment Agreement: Under $50,000 in combined tax/penalties/interest, pay within 72 months, no financial disclosure required. Larger balances require full financial analysis (Form 433-A/433-B).

Q83	What is Currently Not Collectible (CNC) status?
IRS may suspend collection of a tax debt when the taxpayer can demonstrate they have no ability to pay even basic living expenses if forced to pay the tax. IRS continues to assess interest and penalties during CNC status. Statute of Limitations continues to run. IRS reviews periodically and may remove CNC status if financial situation improves.

Q84	What is an Offer in Compromise (OIC)?
IRS program allowing taxpayers to settle tax debt for less than the full amount owed. Based on doubt as to collectibility (IRS cannot collect the full amount), doubt as to liability (dispute the tax), or effective tax administration (full payment would cause economic hardship). Must be current on all filing obligations and estimated taxes. OIC amount based on reasonable collection potential (assets + future income).

Q85	What are IRMAA surcharges and how do they relate to tax preparation?
Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount: Additional Medicare Part B and Part D premium surcharges for higher-income beneficiaries. Based on MAGI from 2 years prior (2024 surcharges based on 2022 MAGI). Single over $103,000 | MFJ over $206,000 trigger surcharges. Reported on Form 8959 if applicable. Tax planning to reduce MAGI (Roth conversions, capital gain timing) can reduce or eliminate IRMAA.

Q86	What is the credit for federal tax on fuels (Form 4136)?
Allows refund of federal excise tax paid on fuel used for off-highway business purposes (farm equipment, off-road machinery) or in vehicles sold on certain routes. Relatively uncommon but important for agricultural clients and contractors.

Q87	What is a Passive Activity and what are the two categories?
Passive activities are business activities in which the taxpayer does NOT materially participate. Two categories: (1) Trade or business activities with no material participation, (2) Rental activities (presumed passive regardless of participation, except for real estate professionals). Passive losses can only offset passive income — cannot offset active income or portfolio income. Material participation: generally requires working 500+ hours in the activity.

Q88	What is the NIIT (Net Investment Income Tax) and what income does it cover?
3.8% tax on the lesser of: net investment income OR the amount by which MAGI exceeds $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ). Investment income includes: interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income (unless from real estate professional), royalties, passive activity income. Does NOT include: wages, self-employment income, active business income, SS benefits, IRA/401(k) distributions, tax-exempt interest.

Q89	What is the additional 0.9% Medicare surtax?
Additional Medicare Tax on earned income (wages + SE income) exceeding $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ). Employers withhold on wages over $200,000 for any employee. If employer withholds too little (because MFJ spouse also has income), the shortfall is calculated on Form 8959 and paid with the return. Note: The threshold is based on combined household income, not per-employer income.

Q90	What is a SIMPLE IRA and how does it differ from a SEP IRA?
SIMPLE IRA: For small employers (100 or fewer employees). Employee salary deferrals allowed — up to $16,000 ($19,500 age 50+) in 2024. Employer required contribution: either 2% nonelective contribution for all eligible employees OR 3% matching. SEP IRA: Only employer contributes — no employee deferrals. Contribution up to 25% of compensation or $69,000 (2024), whichever is less. Easier administration than SIMPLE. Both allow immediate vesting.

Q91	What is an installment sale?
Sale where the seller receives payments in more than one tax year. Gain is reported proportionally as payments are received using the gross profit percentage. Form 6252. Allows spreading of gain recognition and deferral of tax. Cannot be used for inventory, dealer property, or property sold at a loss.

Q92	What is cost segregation in real estate tax planning?
Engineering study that reclassifies components of a commercial or residential building from 39-year or 27.5-year depreciation to shorter lives (5, 7, or 15 years) for components like flooring, fixtures, land improvements. Accelerates depreciation deductions significantly in early years of ownership. Particularly powerful combined with bonus depreciation.

Q93	What is Form 1065 and how does a partnership report income?
Form 1065 is the U.S. Return of Partnership Income — information return filed by partnerships. The partnership itself pays no income tax. Income, deductions, credits, and other items are allocated to partners on Schedule K-1. Partners report K-1 items on their personal returns — ordinary income on Schedule E, capital gains on Schedule D, etc. Partnership must issue K-1s to all partners by the due date of Form 1065.

Q94	What is Form 1120-S and how does an S corporation report income?
Form 1120-S is the U.S. Income Tax Return for an S Corporation — information return. S corps generally pay no corporate income tax. Income passes through to shareholders via Schedule K-1. Unlike partnerships, S corp shareholders who work in the business must receive a reasonable W-2 salary — this prevents converting SE-taxable income into a K-1 distribution that avoids SE tax.

Q95	A taxpayer received $50,000 of unemployment compensation. Is it taxable?
Yes — unemployment compensation is fully taxable as ordinary income at the federal level, reported on Form 1040 Line 1 or as additional income. The state unemployment agency issues Form 1099-G reporting the amount. Taxpayers can elect to have federal income tax withheld from unemployment at 10%. Note: The $10,200 exclusion from COVID-era legislation (2020 only) no longer applies.

Q96	What is the education exclusion for employer-provided assistance?
Under Section 127, employees can exclude up to $5,250 per year of employer-provided educational assistance from income. This applies to both undergraduate and graduate-level education. Qualifying expenses include tuition, fees, books, and supplies. The exclusion applies regardless of whether the education is job-related.

Q97	What is the difference between ordinary income and capital gain for tax purposes?
Ordinary income: Wages, self-employment income, interest, rental income, short-term capital gains — taxed at regular rates (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%). Capital gains (long-term): Assets held over 1 year — taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% rates. The preferential treatment of long-term capital gains is one of the most significant planning opportunities in tax.

Q98	What is the look-through rule for mutual fund distributions?
When a mutual fund sells securities at a profit, it distributes capital gains to shareholders. These distributions retain their character — if the fund held the securities for more than 1 year, they are long-term capital gain distributions (taxed at preferential rates) regardless of how long the shareholder held the fund. Reported in Box 2a of Form 1099-DIV.

Q99	What triggers an AMT audit?
AMT triggers include: exercising Incentive Stock Options (ISOs), claiming accelerated depreciation, large depletion deductions, significant tax-exempt interest from private activity bonds, claiming percentage depletion in excess of cost basis, high itemized deductions especially SALT (which is added back for AMT purposes). Form 6251 calculates whether AMT applies.

Q100	A first-year tax preparer submits a return with an obvious arithmetic error. What ethical obligation exists?
Under Circular 230 Section 10.21, if a practitioner discovers an error in a previously filed return, they must promptly advise the client of the error. The practitioner cannot unilaterally file an amended return — the client must authorize it. The practitioner can withdraw from the engagement if the client refuses to correct a significant error. The practitioner is not required to notify the IRS of the error — only the client.

 
PART 4 — BEHAVIORAL & SOFT SKILLS INTERVIEW QUESTIONS

Top 50 Behavioral Interview Questions
Use the STAR method for all behavioral questions: Situation → Task → Action → Result. Always quantify results where possible.

B1	Tell me about yourself.
Frame it as a professional narrative: 15+ years accounting → discovered US tax → systematic credential-building → ready for remote US tax role. Keep to 90 seconds. End with why this specific firm.

B2	Why do you want to work in US taxation specifically?
Connect to your strengths: analytical rigor, rule-based systems, the intellectual challenge of a complex code, and the professional community of EAs and CPAs. Avoid 'for the money' framing.

B3	Tell me about a time you caught an error that others missed.
Use your zero-penalty record. At Variofil: caught a $240K+ A/R discrepancy through systematic reconciliation. Show the process you used, not just the outcome.

B4	How do you stay current with changing tax laws?
Name specific resources: IRS.gov, Rev. Proc., AICPA updates, EA continuing education CPE, NTA blog, tax professional newsletters. Mention how you applied a recent law change.

B5	Describe a time you had a heavy workload and multiple deadlines.
Tax season scenario — reference managing 100+ accounts at Incuse Group. Show prioritization: what you did first, what you delegated, how you managed client communication.

B6	Tell me about a time you made a mistake at work.
Choose a genuine but minor error. Show what you discovered it, how you fixed it before it had impact, and the process you implemented to prevent recurrence. Never say 'I can't think of any.'

B7	How do you explain complex tax concepts to clients who are not tax professionals?
Give a concrete example: explaining the self-employment tax to a first-time Schedule C client. Use analogies, check for understanding, and follow up in writing.

B8	Describe your experience working independently without direct supervision.
Your India-based remote role with Incuse Group serving international clients is perfect for this. Show specific self-management strategies: daily logs, proactive communication, structured checklist workflows.

B9	Why should we hire you over other India-based tax preparers?
Credential stack (EA Parts 1+3, PTIN, GoSystem, Drake, QBO, Xero), 15+ years of accuracy-first accounting discipline, Intuit Academy 90%+ score across all 13 return types, and commitment to continuous learning.

B10	What is your biggest professional weakness?
Choose a real one that is also improvable: 'I am still building my hands-on US tax client experience. I compensate by being extremely systematic about self-study and simulation.' Then name what you are doing about it.

B11	How do you handle a situation where you disagree with your manager's approach on a tax issue?
Frame as professional dialogue: present your position with technical support (IRC section, Rev. Proc., court case), listen to their reasoning, defer respectfully if they have more information, and document the discussion.

B12	Describe a time you improved a process.
At Variofil: built a 10-person accounting team and implemented a reconciliation process that reduced errors. Quantify the improvement. Processes you built → outcomes you achieved.

B13	How do you prioritize when you have five returns due at the same time?
Sort by complexity, due date, and client communication needs. Complex returns first (more time needed). Communicate proactively if any deadline is at risk. Never miss a deadline without warning.

B14	What motivates you in your work?
Intellectual precision + client impact. The EA designation represents mastery of one of the most complex bodies of law that affects every American — that combination motivates you.

B15	Tell me about a time you had to learn something completely new very quickly.
Your entire US tax transition: learned a different country's tax code from scratch, passed two EA exams, completed all 13 Intuit Academy returns, trained on multiple software platforms — in approximately 18 months.

B16	How do you handle a client who is upset about their tax bill?
Listen first without interrupting. Explain the calculation transparently. Identify any planning opportunities for next year. Do not promise outcomes you cannot deliver. Document the conversation.

B17	Describe your quality control process for a tax return.

Top 20 EA Exam-Relevant Interview Questions
These questions directly test knowledge from EA Parts 1 and 3 — areas your passed exams should make you strong. Do not underperform here.

EA1	What is Circular 230 and what does it govern?
31 CFR Part 10 — Treasury Regulations governing practice before the IRS. Covers: who can practice, standards for written advice, conflict of interest rules, fee structures, and disciplinary sanctions (disbarment, suspension, censure). Every practitioner with a PTIN must comply.

EA2	What are the three types of IRS examinations and what triggers each?
Correspondence Audit: IRS mails questions about specific items — most common, handled by mail or fax. Office Audit: Taxpayer comes to IRS office. Field Audit: IRS agent comes to taxpayer's home or business — most comprehensive, for complex returns or businesses. Triggers: statistical selection, related party examinations, specific high-risk items (EIC, large charitable deductions, cash businesses).

EA3	What is the Collection Due Process (CDP) hearing?
Formal process taxpayers can request within 30 days of receiving a Final Notice of Intent to Levy or Notice of Federal Tax Lien filing. Taxpayer can propose collection alternatives (installment agreement, OIC, currently not collectible, innocent spouse). If CDP result is unsatisfactory, taxpayer can appeal to Tax Court. The levy is held during the CDP process.

EA4	What is the difference between an audit reconsideration and an appeal?
Audit Reconsideration: Informal process to ask IRS to reconsider an assessment made after an audit, typically when taxpayer has new documentation they could not provide during audit or believes the IRS made an error. Does NOT suspend collection. Appeals: Formal administrative process within the IRS Office of Appeals — independent from examination. Suspends tax assessment while under appeal. Before Tax Court.

EA5	Explain the Innocent Spouse Relief options.
Three types: Traditional Innocent Spouse (Form 8857): Relief from joint liability for understatement of tax due to erroneous items of the other spouse. Must not have known or had reason to know. Separation of Liability: Allocates additional tax liability between spouses proportionally. Equitable Relief: When neither of the above applies — IRS considers whether it would be unfair to hold the requesting spouse liable. Must be requested within 2 years (traditional/separation) or collection period (equitable).

EA6	What is the IRS Appeals process?
After examination, taxpayer receives 30-day letter offering the right to appeal within 30 days to the Office of Appeals. If not appealed or after Appeals, IRS issues a 90-day letter (Statutory Notice of Deficiency). Taxpayer has 90 days to petition the Tax Court without paying first. After 90 days, tax is assessed and taxpayer can pay then sue for refund in Federal District Court or Court of Claims.

EA7	What powers does an Enrolled Agent have that a non-credentialed preparer does not?
Unlimited representation rights before all IRS offices: examination, collection, Appeals. Can represent taxpayers for returns they did not prepare. Can sign Form 2848. Can appear in CDP hearings and Appeals conferences. Can negotiate installment agreements and OICs on behalf of clients. Can request transcripts, receive confidential information.

EA8	What is the difference between a deficiency and an assessment?
Deficiency: The amount by which the tax IRS claims the taxpayer owes exceeds the tax shown on the return (or zero if no return filed). Assessment: The formal recording of a tax liability on the IRS records — once assessed, IRS can begin collection. IRS generally cannot assess until 90-day letter period expires or Tax Court decision becomes final.

EA9	What is the difference between FICA and SECA?
FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act): Applies to employees and employers — 7.65% each (6.2% SS + 1.45% Medicare). SECA (Self-Employment Contributions Act): Applies to self-employed individuals — 15.3% total (they pay both halves). 50% of SECA is deductible above the line. Both fund Social Security and Medicare. SS portion applies only up to $168,600 (2024 wage base).

EA10	A taxpayer receives a CP2000 notice but believes it is wrong. What steps do you take?
1) Request the full 60 days for response. 2) Gather all documentation supporting the return position. 3) File Form 2848 to be able to discuss with IRS on client's behalf. 4) Write a formal disagreement response letter with all supporting documentation. 5) If not resolved, the next step is an examination or formal protest to Appeals. The CP2000 is not an audit — it's a proposed adjustment. A clear documented response resolves most CP2000s.

EA11	What is Trust Fund Recovery Penalty (TFRP)?
IRS can assess a 100% penalty personally against responsible persons who willfully fail to collect or pay over trust fund taxes (payroll withholding — federal income tax, SS, Medicare withheld from employee wages). 'Responsible person' includes anyone with authority to determine financial priorities — owner, officer, accountant with check-signing authority. Cannot be discharged in bankruptcy.

EA12	What is the difference between tax avoidance and tax evasion from a Circular 230 perspective?
Tax avoidance: Legal. A practitioner has the obligation to advise clients on legal methods to reduce taxes. Tax evasion: Illegal. A practitioner who assists with tax evasion violates Circular 230 and faces disbarment, criminal referral, and personal liability. Key test: Is the taxpayer legally entitled to the claimed position? Is it disclosed if uncertain?

EA13	What are the standards for a covered opinion under Circular 230?
IMPORTANT: The 'covered opinion' standards in Sec 10.35 were removed in 2014. Current standard (Sec 10.37): Written advice must be based on reasonable factual and legal assumptions, consider relevant facts, apply law correctly, not depend on unlikely conclusions. Standard is 'reasonable practitioner' standard — would a reasonable practitioner with similar expertise give the same advice?

EA14	What is an Offer in Compromise and what are the eligibility tests?
OIC settles tax debt for less than full amount. Three grounds: (1) Doubt as to Collectibility (DATC): IRS cannot collect the full amount — most common. Reasonable Collection Potential (RCP) = assets + future income. (2) Doubt as to Liability (DATL): Taxpayer believes tax is wrong. (3) Effective Tax Administration (ETA): Tax is correct and could theoretically be collected but would cause economic hardship or be inequitable. Must be current on all filing and payment obligations to apply.

EA15	What is the Collection Statute of Limitations?
IRS generally has 10 years from the date of assessment to collect unpaid taxes (IRC Section 6502). The period is tolled (paused) during: pending OIC, bankruptcy, installment agreement in certain situations, taxpayer outside US for 6+ months, and CDP hearing. Taxpayers should NEVER voluntarily sign a waiver of this period without legal advice.

EA16	What is a Statutory Notice of Deficiency and what are the taxpayer's options?
Also called a 90-day letter or SNOD. The IRS sends this after exam or AUR process if the taxpayer does not agree. Taxpayer has 90 days (150 days if foreign) to petition the US Tax Court WITHOUT PAYING the disputed tax first. If the taxpayer does not petition Tax Court within 90 days, the tax is assessed and the IRS begins collection. The Tax Court is the only forum where taxpayer can contest tax WITHOUT paying first.

EA17	What is the Whistleblower program and how does it relate to tax enforcement?
Under IRC 7623, individuals who report tax fraud to the IRS may be entitled to a reward of 15-30% of the additional tax collected if the amount in dispute exceeds $2 million ($1 million in cases involving individuals). Filed using Form 211. The IRS Whistleblower Office processes claims. Awards are taxable income to the whistleblower.

EA18	How does the Taxpayer Bill of Rights affect your practice?
Ten rights established by Congress in IRC Section 7803(a)(3): Right to be informed, right to quality service, right to pay no more than correct amount, right to challenge IRS position, right to appeal in an independent forum, right to finality, right to privacy, right to confidentiality, right to retain representation, right to a fair and just tax system. As an EA, you help clients exercise all of these rights.

EA19	What are the EA continuing education requirements?
72 hours of CE per 3-year enrollment cycle. Minimum 16 hours per year, including at least 2 hours of ethics per year. CE must be from IRS-approved providers. Record-keeping required. CE can be in-person, online, or self-study. Failure to complete CE results in non-renewal of enrollment.

EA20	What is the difference between a tax return preparer and an enrolled agent from the IRS's perspective?
Tax Return Preparer: Anyone who prepares returns for compensation — must have PTIN. Non-credentialed preparers have very limited IRS representation rights. Enrolled Agent: Specifically licensed by the Treasury to practice before the IRS — highest credential from the IRS perspective. Subject to Circular 230, competency requirements, CE requirements, and ethical standards. EA status is federal (nationwide) — not state-limited like CPA.

WHY THEY ASK: Baseline competency test. If you cannot do this basic 1040, no amount of soft skills will get you the job.
STRONG ANSWER: 
I'd start with income entry on Form 1040. Line 1a: $90,000 W-2 wages. Line 2b: $2,500 taxable interest from 1099-INT. Line 3b: $3,000 total dividends (but $2,800 qualifies for preferential rates). Then standard deduction of $14,600 for single in 2024. Taxable income = $80,900. Tax calculation: ordinary income (wages + $200 non-qualified dividends + interest) vs preferential rates for $2,800 qualified dividends. The qualified dividends would be taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% based on their total taxable income. At $80,900 taxable income, the 15% long-term capital gain rate applies since they are above $47,025. So qualified dividends of $2,800 × 15% = $420 tax on dividends. Remaining ordinary income taxed at regular bracket rates. I'd also verify there's no AMT, no additional Medicare tax (under $200K threshold), and check for any credits (education, retirement savings).
WEAK ANSWER: I'd enter all the income and calculate the tax. Qualified dividends get a lower rate.
SCORING NOTES: Good answer: 9-10. Weak answer: 2-3. Must demonstrate line-by-line process, must know 2024 standard deduction, must explain qualified dividend rate calculation.

MQ4: A self-employed client has net Schedule C income of $75,000. Calculate their self-employment tax and explain how it affects their Form 1040.
WHY THEY ASK: SE tax calculation is a core competency for any preparer working with self-employed clients.
STRONG ANSWER: 
For SE tax calculation: First, I multiply net Schedule C income of $75,000 by 92.35% — this adjustment represents the employer-equivalent deduction, reducing the SE tax base to $69,263. Then I multiply $69,263 by 15.3% (the full SE tax rate which covers both the employee and employer shares of Social Security and Medicare) = approximately $10,597 in SE tax. This is reported on Schedule SE. Now, how does this affect Form 1040? The $10,597 SE tax is added to the taxpayer's total tax on Form 1040. However, 50% of SE tax — approximately $5,298 — is deducted above the line on Schedule 1, Line 15, reducing AGI. This is the employer-equivalent portion. So the net economic impact: the taxpayer pays $10,597 in SE tax but gets a deduction worth approximately $5,298 × their marginal rate. At 22% bracket, that's a $1,166 tax savings from the deduction.
WEAK ANSWER: SE tax is 15.3% on self-employment income. They'd owe $11,475 and can deduct half.
SCORING NOTES: Good answer: 9-10. Weak answer: 4-5. Must know the 92.35% multiplier, must explain the above-the-line deduction for 50% of SE tax, and demonstrate the Schedule SE → Schedule 1 → Form 1040 flow.
FOLLOW-UP: The client wants to contribute to a SEP IRA to reduce taxes. What is the maximum they can contribute?

MQ5: A client receives a CP2000 notice from the IRS saying they did not report $12,000 of income from a 1099-NEC. They claim they DID report it. What do you do?
WHY THEY ASK: Tests real-world IRS procedure knowledge and professional process — not just textbook knowledge.
STRONG ANSWER: 
First, I obtain a Form 2848 Power of Attorney from the client authorizing me to discuss this matter with the IRS. Then I pull the original filed return and verify whether the $12,000 was in fact reported. A common reason for CP2000 mismatches: the income WAS reported but on a different form or line — for example, if reported on Schedule C under a business name rather than as a 1099-NEC exactly matching the IRS records. I'd review the actual 1099-NEC to confirm the payer's information and the amount. Then I'd compare to the filed return to find where the income was reported. Once I have documentation, I respond to the CP2000 within the 60-day deadline (or request an extension if needed) with a written explanation and supporting documentation showing the income WAS included in the return — for example, showing it as part of gross receipts on Schedule C, Line 1. The response should include a cover letter explaining the discrepancy, a copy of the relevant Schedule C section, and the 1099-NEC itself. If the IRS disagrees, we can escalate to the Appeals division.
WEAK ANSWER: I'd tell the client to call the IRS and explain that they already reported the income.
SCORING NOTES: Good answer: 9-10. Weak answer: 2-3. Must mention Form 2848, documentation process, 60-day deadline, written response, and escalation path.
FOLLOW-UP: What if you find that the client actually MISSED this income and it truly was not reported? How does your advice change?
